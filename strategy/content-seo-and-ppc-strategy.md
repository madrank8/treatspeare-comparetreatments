## 1. Executive Summary & Strategic Thesis

### 1.1 The thesis: compete on PPC, win on SEO

We are entering a market where the leading competitor has built a large, expensive, and *single-channel* business. The strategic opportunity is not to copy them — it is to **match them on the channel they dominate while attacking the flank they have left completely undefended.**

This is a **dual-engine strategy**:

- **Engine 1 — Paid search (immediate revenue):** Match and undercut the competitor on paid search, with **Bing/Microsoft Ads as the primary channel** and Google Ads secondary. Paid search produces revenue from day one (once compliance clearance is in place) and funds the business while Engine 2 compounds.
- **Engine 2 — Organic SEO + AI-search visibility (the moat):** Build the deep, E-E-A-T-strong topical authority the competitor has *entirely neglected*. Organic traffic compounds, does not get billed per click, and progressively lowers blended customer-acquisition cost (CAC). This is the durable competitive advantage and the reason this business is worth more in 24 months than the competitor's is today.

The one-line version: **The competitor rents its entire audience. We will rent for cash flow and own for equity.**

### 1.2 Why the competitor is vulnerable — the hard data

Ahrefs and project research on comparetreatments.com reveals a striking imbalance:

| Metric | comparetreatments.com | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Organic keywords ranking | **3** | Effectively zero organic presence |
| Organic visits / month | **~4** | Negligible — no SEO investment |
| Paid keywords | **364** | Heavy paid search program |
| Paid visits / month | **~36,862** | Their entire traffic base is rented |
| Estimated Google Ads spend / month | **~$247,000** | ~$3M/year on Google alone, plus Bing |
| Paid landing pages | **520** | Mature, well-developed PPC infrastructure |
| Also running | **Bing / Microsoft Ads** | Second paid channel confirmed |

**What this tells us:**

1. **Their moat is 100% rented.** If their ad accounts are suspended (a real risk in this heavily policed healthcare vertical), or if CPCs rise, their traffic — and revenue — collapses overnight. There is no organic floor.
2. **Their organic flank is wide open.** Three keywords. Any competitor that invests seriously in content and technical SEO can take the entire organic SERP for this category with relatively modest, compounding effort.
3. **Their content is thin and exploitable.** Category hubs run ~1,500 words of generic, templated SEO body text with a single copy-pasted 5-question FAQ reused across pages. Review pages are thin generic prose with **no named author, no medical reviewer, no visible scoring, no first-hand testing, and unresolved `[1]`–`[4]` citation placeholders.** There is **no structured data (schema) anywhere on the site.** For a YMYL (Your Money or Your Life) health vertical, this is the weakest possible content posture. Google's Quality Rater Guidelines and the 2024 Search API leak both reward exactly the signals they lack: demonstrated expertise, named authorship, primary-source citation, first-hand experience, and content depth.
4. **They have built our paid playbook for us.** 520 landing pages and 364 paid keywords are a publicly visible blueprint of what converts in this market. We can study their ad copy and landing pages, match their structure, and improve on message match and compliance.

### 1.3 The 2X play

To be *better* than comparetreatments.com — not just a clone — we will:

- **Match them on paid search**, but lead with **Bing** (cheaper CPCs, they are already proving it works there, less auction competition than Google) and run Google as the scaling channel.
- **Build the SEO + AI-search moat they skipped.** Genuine topical authority across all categories, real E-E-A-T, real review methodology, complete schema coverage. This is a 6–18 month compounding investment that structurally lowers CAC.
- **Out-trust them on conversion.** They use a fake "1,804 people compared this week" urgency counter and unresolved citations. We win on *honest* trust signals: real, documented methodology; dated updates; named experts; transparent affiliate disclosure. In a YMYL vertical, credibility converts — and it is also what gets us cited by AI engines.

The result over 12 months: a paid program at parity-or-better economics, *plus* a growing organic channel the competitor cannot quickly replicate, driving blended CAC down every quarter.

## 2. PPC / Paid Search Strategy

Paid search is Engine 1 — the cash-flow engine. It must be live as fast as compliance allows, structured tightly, and measured ruthlessly on profit per click, not vanity traffic.

### 2.1 Task #1, non-negotiable, before any spend: LegitScript certification

**You cannot advertise healthcare on Google Ads or Microsoft Advertising without LegitScript Healthcare Merchant Certification.** This applies to comparison and affiliate sites in the telehealth space — not just to pharmacies themselves. Attempting to run paid healthcare campaigns without it results in account suspension, and suspensions in this vertical are difficult to reverse.

| Item | Detail |
|---|---|
| What | LegitScript Healthcare Merchant Certification |
| Cost — application | **$975 one-time** |
| Cost — annual | **$2,150 / year per website** |
| Required by | Google Ads (online pharmacy/telemedicine advertiser policy) and Microsoft Advertising (also accepts NABP as an alternative) |
| Typical timeline | Plan for **4–8 weeks** from application to certification — varies with how clean the site and business documentation are |
| Action | **Begin the LegitScript application on Day 1 of the project.** It is the long pole in the tent. Every paid-channel milestone is downstream of it. |

**Implication for sequencing:** the certification clock starts before the site is even fully built. Stand up a compliant, complete-enough site (real legal pages, affiliate disclosure, contact info, about/methodology pages, no prohibited claims) early specifically so the LegitScript review can proceed. Budget the $975 + first-year $2,150 as a launch cost. Do not let PPC planning wait on certification — do all campaign build-out, keyword research, and landing-page production *in parallel* so we can flip campaigns live the day approval lands.

### 2.2 Channel strategy: Bing primary, Google secondary

This is a deliberate inversion of the default "Google first" instinct, and it is core to the 2X thesis.

**Lead with Microsoft Advertising (Bing Ads) because:**

- **Lower CPCs.** Bing CPCs in most US verticals run materially below Google — often 30–50% cheaper — which directly improves EPC-to-CPC margin.
- **The competitor is already there.** Project research confirms comparetreatments.com runs Microsoft Ads. They have validated that Bing converts in this market. We do not need to test whether the channel works — only to execute it better.
- **Less auction competition.** Fewer sophisticated advertisers compete on Bing, so position and impression share are cheaper to win.
- **Audience skew.** The Bing audience skews older and higher-income — favorable for several of our highest-value categories (TRT, HRT, GLP-1 weight loss, blood testing, advanced wellness).

**Run Google Ads as the scaling channel because:**

- Far larger total search volume — necessary for scale once unit economics are proven.
- Better measurement, audience, and automation tooling.
- We accept higher Google CPCs *only* on ad groups that have proven profitable on Bing first.

**Operating rule:** prove an ad group profitable on Bing, then port the winning structure to Google. Bing is the cheaper laboratory; Google is the scale-up. Do not launch both simultaneously at full budget — Bing first, validate, then expand.

> **Critical Bing compliance note:** Microsoft Advertising's healthcare policy is **more restrictive than Google's.** Bing prohibits "ads related to sensitive health conditions targeting consumers," heavily restricts weight-loss product claims, and forbids language that implies direct health targeting — phrases like *"your condition,"* *"your symptoms,"* *"treat your ED."* Because Bing is our *primary* channel, **all ad copy and landing pages must be written to the stricter Bing standard from the start** (see §2.5). Writing to Bing's bar means Google compliance is automatically satisfied.

### 2.3 Campaign structure

Structure every account by intent tier. Each tier has different economics, different copy, and different compliance exposure.

| Campaign type | What it targets | Why | Priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Branded** | "comparetreatments [2.0 brand name]", our own brand terms | Cheapest clicks, highest intent, defends against conquest. Low volume but essential. | Launch immediately, tiny budget |
| **Category / non-branded** | Generic category demand: "best telehealth weight loss", "online ED treatment", "telehealth TRT" | The core volume driver. Highest spend share. | Primary — bulk of budget |
| **Competitor conquesting** | Competitor brand names — telehealth providers *and* comparison sites (including comparetreatments.com itself) | Intercept high-intent users shopping a named brand. Higher CPCs, strong intent. Strict trademark/policy care required. | Phase 2, after category proven |
| **Long-tail / specific** | Drug-specific and modifier queries: "compounded semaglutide cost", "tirzepatide vs semaglutide telehealth", "TRT clinic online cost" | Cheaper CPCs, higher conversion, less competition. Strong margin. | Phase 1–2, expand continuously |

### 2.4 Landing-page strategy

The competitor has 520 paid landing pages. We do **not** send paid traffic to general content pages — paid traffic gets **dedicated, conversion-optimized, compliance-cleared PPC landing pages**, separate from (but consistent with) the SEO content.

**Anatomy of a high-converting, compliant comparison landing page:**

1. **Headline with message match.** The headline echoes the ad and the query. If the ad group is "Compounded Semaglutide / Cost," the LP headline addresses cost of compounded semaglutide options. Compliance: describe the *comparison*, not a promise about the user's body.
2. **Above-the-fold comparison table.** The core asset: 3–6 vetted telehealth providers compared on the dimensions that drive the decision — starting price, what's included (consultation, labs, medication, ongoing support), speed to first appointment, subscription terms, and our editorial score. Each row has a clear, tracked CTA.
3. **Honest scoring + transparent methodology link.** Show real editorial scores and link to a documented methodology page. This is a *conversion* asset and an *AI-citation* asset — it is also the opposite of the competitor's fake urgency counter.
4. **Trust strip.** "Independently reviewed," "Updated [date]," named editor/medical reviewer, transparent affiliate-disclosure line. Real, dated, attributable.
5. **Concise, compliant supporting copy.** A short, factual section on how the category works and how to choose — enough to inform and satisfy the click intent, not a 1,500-word SEO wall on a paid LP.
6. **Compliant FAQ.** 4–6 genuinely useful, query-specific questions (NOT one copy-pasted block reused site-wide).
7. **Clear, conspicuous affiliate disclosure** near every outbound affiliate link, not buried in the footer.
8. **Repeated, tracked CTAs** down the page, all to qualified affiliate destinations.

**Message-match rule:** ad group → ad copy → LP headline → comparison table focus must form one unbroken chain. Mismatches waste spend and depress Quality Score.

#### Compliance guardrails for ad copy AND landing pages

Write everything to the **stricter Microsoft standard.** This table is the working rule set for copywriters:

| ❌ Do NOT say | ✅ Say instead |
|---|---|
| "Treat your ED" / "your condition" / "your symptoms" | "Compare ED treatment options" / "Online ED treatment providers compared" |
| "Lose 20 lbs fast" / specific weight-loss outcome claims | "Compare telehealth weight-loss programs" / "GLP-1 providers, compared" |
| "FDA-approved compounded semaglutide" | "Compare telehealth providers offering compounded semaglutide" — never claim compounded drugs are FDA-approved or equivalent to Ozempic/Wegovy/Zepbound |
| "Same as Ozempic / Wegovy / Zepbound" | Refer to compounded formulations as compounded; do not assert brand-drug equivalence |
| "Cure," "guaranteed results," "miracle" | "Options," "compare," "what to consider," "how it works" |
| Implying we diagnose, prescribe, or provide care | "We compare independent telehealth providers" — we are an editorial comparison site, not a provider |
| Targeting/implying a sensitive condition the user has | Speak to the *category* generically; never imply we know the user's health status |

**Hard rules:**
- **No unsubstantiated health claims** anywhere. Every health statement must be factual and, in content, sourced.
- **Compounded GLP-1s:** never "FDA-approved," never equivalent to brand drugs. The FDA issued 100+ warning letters to telehealth GLP-1 marketers in September 2025 and 30 more in March 2026 — assume scrutiny is active and ongoing. Keep all GLP-1 copy conservative and factual.
- **Affiliate disclosure** clear and conspicuous near every affiliate link (FTC requirement).
- **No "your condition / your symptoms" framing** — Microsoft's rule, applied everywhere.
- When in doubt, describe **the comparison and the options**, never make a promise about the individual user's body or outcome.

### 2.5 Tracking & measurement

**Define the conversion precisely.** A page view is not a conversion. The conversion is a **qualified affiliate click or a qualified call** — a user landing on a partner provider with revenue potential.

- **Pay-per-click / affiliate-click conversions:** fire a conversion event on every tracked outbound click to a monetized partner. Pass sub-IDs so revenue can be reconciled per campaign / ad group / keyword.
- **Pay-per-call:** use a **call-tracking provider** (e.g. CallRail, Invoca, or partner-supplied tracking numbers) with dynamic number insertion on landing pages, so calls are attributed to the campaign and keyword that drove them. Many telehealth partners pay per qualified call — this must be measured, not estimated.
- **Server-side / offline conversion import:** where partners report conversions back (commission confirmed, call qualified), import those into Bing and Google so smart bidding optimizes to *revenue*, not raw clicks.

**Target metrics and thresholds:**

| Metric | Definition | Target / threshold |
|---|---|---|
| **CPC** | Cost per click | Channel-dependent; Bing should run well below Google |
| **EPC** | Earnings per click (affiliate revenue ÷ clicks to site) | Must exceed CPC with margin — this is the core unit-economics test |
| **Conversion rate** | Qualified affiliate clicks/calls ÷ LP sessions | Benchmark per category; optimize via message match + LP testing |
| **Revenue per call** | For pay-per-call partners | Track per category; calls often pay more than clicks |
| **ROAS** | Revenue ÷ ad spend | **Minimum 1.5–2.0x to scale an ad group; pause below ~1.2x** pending fixes |
| **Quality Score** | Bing/Google relevance metric | Maintain high via tight ad groups + message match — directly lowers CPC |

**Operating cadence:** review at the **keyword and ad-group level weekly.** Pause or fix anything below the ROAS floor. Reallocate budget toward ad groups beating EPC-to-CPC margin. Treat the account as a portfolio of bets, continuously rebalanced.

### 2.6 Budget framing & phased ramp

Budget should ramp with *proof*, not ambition. The competitor's ~$247K/month Google spend is their steady state, not our starting line — we earn our way up.

| Phase | Timing | Focus | Spend posture |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Phase 0 — Compliance & build** | Months 0–2 | LegitScript application; site build to a compliant baseline; campaign + LP build-out; keyword research | **$0 media.** Sunk cost: ~$3,125 LegitScript (app + year 1) |
| **Phase 1 — Bing pilot** | Months 2–4 (post-certification) | Branded + 2–3 category campaigns (lead with Weight Loss/GLP-1, ED) on **Bing only.** Validate EPC > CPC. | Controlled daily caps; small, disciplined budget; scale only what clears the ROAS floor |
| **Phase 2 — Bing scale + Google entry** | Months 4–8 | Expand Bing to all profitable categories + long-tail; launch winning structures on Google; add competitor conquesting | Scale spend in step with proven ROAS; Bing remains the cheaper core |
| **Phase 3 — Full dual-channel** | Months 8–12+ | All categories live on both channels; aggressive conquesting; budget shifts to wherever marginal ROAS is highest | Scale toward and past competitor parity *only* while unit economics hold |

**Guiding principle:** every dollar of paid budget must clear the ROAS floor before it scales. We are not buying traffic — we are buying *profitable* traffic, and growing the budget only as fast as the data earns it. Meanwhile every month of paid revenue is partly reinvested into Engine 2 (SEO), which lowers blended CAC and reduces dependence on paid over time.

## 3. SEO Strategy — The Organic Moat

This is Engine 2 — the durable advantage. The competitor has **3 organic keywords.** The entire organic SERP for US telehealth comparison is effectively unclaimed. We claim it with genuine topical authority, real E-E-A-T, and complete technical SEO.

### 3.1 The topical authority approach

Google rewards sites that demonstrate **comprehensive, expert coverage of a topic** — not isolated pages. We will build **topical clusters**: each category gets a hub page plus a dense web of supporting pages (sub-hubs, brand reviews, comparisons, "best-for" pages, and educational articles) that interlink tightly.

The competitor publishes ~1,500-word templated hubs with a recycled FAQ and nothing beneath them. That is not topical authority — it is a thin page with no cluster. We out-build them on **depth, breadth, and demonstrated expertise.** Topical authority compounds: as a cluster fills in, every page in it ranks better, because the site proves it genuinely owns the subject.

### 3.2 The page-type taxonomy

Every category cluster is built from the same repeatable page types. This makes production scalable and internal linking systematic.

| Page type | Intent | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Category hub** | Commercial investigation | The cluster's pillar. "Best telehealth [category] providers." Comparison-led, links down to everything in the cluster. |
| **Drug / subtopic hub** | Commercial + informational | Sub-pillars for major drugs or sub-treatments (e.g. semaglutide, tirzepatide within Weight Loss). |
| **Brand review pages** | Commercial investigation | Deep, first-hand-grounded review of one telehealth provider. Score, methodology, pros/cons, pricing. |
| **Comparison / "vs" pages** | Commercial investigation | Head-to-head: "Brand A vs Brand B," "semaglutide vs tirzepatide." High commercial intent, high conversion. |
| **"Best X for Y" pages** | Commercial investigation | Segmented best-of: "best telehealth weight loss for [budget/need]." Captures long-tail commercial demand. |
| **Informational / educational articles** | Informational | The top-of-funnel cluster: how treatments work, costs, side effects, eligibility. Builds topical authority, earns AI citations, feeds internal links down to commercial pages. |

We cover **all three intent tiers** — commercial investigation, transactional, and informational — because Google needs to see we own the *whole* topic, and because informational content is what AI engines cite (see §4).

### 3.4 On-page & content quality standard — E-E-A-T

This is YMYL content. Google holds health content to the highest E-E-A-T bar, and it is *exactly* where the competitor is weakest. Every page must meet this standard:

- **Named authors with real bios.** Every page has a real, credentialed author with a linked bio page (Person schema, credentials, experience). No anonymous content.
- **"Medically reviewed by" — real medical reviewers.** Health-claim pages carry a "Medically reviewed by [Name, credentials]" line, with a dated review and a linked reviewer bio. Engage licensed clinicians (MD, DO, NP, PharmD, RD as appropriate) for this. This single signal is something the competitor entirely lacks.
- **Primary-source citations — resolved, not placeholders.** Cite FDA, NIH, peer-reviewed journals, CDC, and clinical guidelines. **Every citation must resolve to a real, linked source** — the competitor's unresolved `[1]`–`[4]` placeholders are a credibility failure we exploit by doing it properly.
- **Genuine review methodology.** Publish a real, documented methodology page explaining how providers are evaluated and scored (pricing, included services, clinician access, transparency, user experience, etc.). Reviews must reflect **actual first-hand testing** — sign up, evaluate the intake, document the experience. First-hand experience is the "Experience" in E-E-A-T and is increasingly decisive.
- **Content depth that beats the competitor.** Where they publish ~1,500 generic words, we publish genuinely comprehensive pages — but depth means *useful coverage*, not padding. Answer the full question set, cover the intent completely, include comparison data, real pricing, and decision guidance.
- **Honest, dated freshness.** Every page shows a real "last updated" date and is genuinely kept current — critical for YMYL and for the fast-moving GLP-1 space.
- **Transparent affiliate disclosure** and a clear statement that we are an independent comparison site, not a healthcare provider.

### 3.5 Internal linking & site structure

Structure the site to *broadcast* topical authority:

- **Hub-and-spoke per cluster.** The category hub links down to every subtopic hub, brand review, comparison page, and article in its cluster; each spoke links back up to the hub. This concentrates relevance and authority.
- **Contextual cross-linking within a cluster.** Articles link to relevant comparison and brand pages ("How GLP-1s work" → "Best GLP-1 telehealth providers"). Informational content feeds link equity *and users* down toward commercial pages.
- **Sensible cross-cluster links** where genuinely relevant (Men's Health hub ↔ ED, TRT, Hair Loss).
- **Clean URL hierarchy** mirroring the cluster structure: `/[category]/`, `/[category]/[subtopic]/`, `/reviews/[provider]/`, `/[category]/[comparison-slug]/`.
- **Breadcrumbs** on every page (with BreadcrumbList schema).

## 4. GEO / AI Search Visibility

A growing share of health research now happens in AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini — not the classic blue links. Generative Engine Optimization (GEO) is now a first-class channel, and the competitor — with 3 organic keywords and no schema — is completely invisible to it. We design for AI citation from day one.

**How to get cited by AI engines:**

- **Extractable, self-contained answers.** Lead sections with a direct, concise answer to the question, then expand. AI engines lift clean, quotable passages — write so a 2–4 sentence chunk fully answers the query on its own. Use clear question-style H2s/H3s that mirror real queries.
- **Structured data everywhere.** FAQPage, MedicalWebPage, Product/Review, ItemList (§3.6) make content machine-readable and far easier for AI systems to parse, attribute, and cite.
- **Primary-source citations.** AI engines preferentially cite content that itself cites authoritative primary sources (FDA, NIH, peer-reviewed studies). Our resolved, real citations are both an E-E-A-T signal and a GEO signal.
- **Genuine first-hand experience.** "We tested," "we signed up," "we measured" — original, experiential content that cannot be synthesized from other pages is disproportionately valuable to AI engines seeking authentic, non-derivative sources. This is the strongest GEO differentiator and the hardest for competitors to copy.
- **Clear entity definitions.** Define terms cleanly ("Compounded semaglutide is...") — AI systems favor unambiguous, well-structured definitional content.
- **Comparison data in structured formats.** Tables and clearly structured lists of providers/prices/features are highly extractable for the comparative queries AI users ask ("what's the cheapest way to get semaglutide online").
- **Freshness.** AI engines favor current content — our real, dated update cadence (§5) helps here too.
- **Be technically reachable.** Allow AI crawlers; keep the site fast and clean.

GEO is not a separate workstream — it is the natural payoff of doing SEO content *right*: extractable structure, real sourcing, genuine first-hand experience, complete schema. Do §3 well and §4 largely follows.

## 6. Conversion Strategy

The job of every page is to send qualified, well-informed users to telehealth partners — and to do it with **honest** trust signals that also satisfy YMYL E-E-A-T and AI-citation criteria. We explicitly reject the competitor's fake-urgency tactics.

### 6.1 How each page type funnels to affiliate clicks

- **Comparison pages** convert hardest: the user is choosing. Lead with the comparison table; every row has a clear, tracked CTA to the provider; reinforce with honest scores, transparent methodology, and concise decision guidance. Repeat tracked CTAs down the page.
- **Brand review pages** convert decided users: a user reading "[Provider] review" is close to choosing. Deliver an honest, thorough, scored review with a clear CTA — and cross-link to the relevant comparison page so undecided users can weigh alternatives (and we still capture the click).
- **Informational articles** convert by *education then routing*: answer the question fully and genuinely, then contextually link down to the relevant comparison/brand pages ("Ready to compare providers? See our [best GLP-1 telehealth providers]"). Top-of-funnel users enter the cluster and move toward commercial pages — and these articles are also the AI-citation and topical-authority engine.

### 6.2 Trust signals — honest, and therefore better

The competitor uses a fake "1,804 people compared this week" urgency counter and ships unresolved `[1]`–`[4]` citations. In a YMYL vertical this is a liability — it erodes trust with users *and* signals low quality to Google and AI engines. **We do the opposite, and it converts better:**

| Competitor (fake / thin) | Us (honest / real) |
|---|---|
| Fabricated "1,804 people this week" counter | Real, documented review methodology page |
| Unresolved `[1]`–`[4]` citation placeholders | Real, resolved, linked primary-source citations |
| No author, no medical reviewer | Named authors + named medical reviewers with bios |
| No visible scoring | Transparent, methodology-backed editorial scores shown |
| No first-hand testing | Genuine first-hand provider testing, documented |
| Stale, undated content | Real, dated "last updated" + medical-review dates |
| Affiliate relationship obscured | Clear, conspicuous affiliate disclosure near every link |

Honest trust signals are not a compliance tax — they are the conversion strategy. In YMYL, credibility *is* what makes a user click through to a provider, and it is simultaneously what earns rankings and AI citations. Trust converts.
